Precedence of Logical Operators: () NOT, AND, OR, ->, <-> 

p -> q means p is sufficient for q, and q is necessary for p.
'q is necessary for p' means Given p -> q, p is true only if q is true.
'p is sufficient for q' means Given p -> q, q is true if p is true.
Converting English to Boolean logic:
  'you know', 'sure', 'good enough', 'therefore' often mean sufficient  
  'must', 'only if', 'in order for' mean necessary 
   AND is expressed in English as 'and', 'but', 'nevertheless'
   Exclusive or (XOR) in English is 'either p or q but not both'.

17. Is the following a correct application of logical equivalences to statement S, where S is defined as: (~p ^ (p v q)) -> q? 
   (~p ^ (p v q)) -> q  == ~(~p ^ (p v q)) v q        by Implication elimination
                        == (p v ~(p v q)) v q         by De Morgan's
                        == (p v (~p ^ ~q)) v q        by De Morgan's
                        == ((p v ~p) ^ (p v ~q)) v q  by Distribution 
                        == (T ^ (p v ~q)) v q         by Negation Law 
                        == (p v ~q) v q               by Identity Law 
                        == p v (~q v q)               by Commutativity
                        == p v T                      by Negation Law 
                        == T                          by Domination Law
